Chapter 13: Field Research & Focus Groups

Understanding Social Life Up Close

What is Field Research?

Often called ethnography or participant-observation, field research is a qualitative method where a researcher directly observes and participates in a natural social setting for an extended period. The goal is to get an "insider's" understanding of a group's culture, norms, and worldview.

The researcher is the primary instrument for collecting and analyzing data.

The Field Research Journey

Conducting field research is a flexible, non-linear process that involves navigating complex social situations. Click each step to learn more.

1. Choosing a Site & Gaining Access

+

The first step is selecting a "field site" where the social life you want to study occurs. Once chosen, you must gain access, which often means negotiating with gatekeepers—key individuals who can grant or deny you entry into the community.

2. Entering the Field & Building Rapport

+

Once you have access, you must establish a social role and build rapport (trust and social connection) with the members of the group. Your level of participation can range from being a complete observer to a complete participant.

3. Observing and Collecting Data

+

The core of field research is careful observation and detailed note-taking. Researchers learn to separate concrete observations from their own interpretations.

  • Jotted Notes: Quick, memory-triggering notes taken in the field.
  • Direct Observation Notes: Detailed, chronological notes of what you saw and heard, written immediately after leaving the field.
  • Inference Notes: Your interpretations and initial analysis of what the observations mean.
  • Personal Notes: Your own feelings and emotional reactions during the research process.

4. The Field Interview

+

Unlike a formal survey interview, a field interview is more like a friendly conversation. It is unstructured, flexible, and aims to get in-depth stories and perspectives from the members in their own words.

5. Leaving the Field

+

Leaving the field can be an emotionally and socially difficult process. The researcher must carefully disengage from the relationships and roles they have built, often over months or even years.

Focus Group Research

A related qualitative technique where a small group of people are brought together for a focused discussion on a specific topic, guided by a trained moderator.

Key Characteristics

  • Typically 6-12 participants per group.
  • Participants are often strangers but share a common characteristic.
  • The moderator keeps the discussion on track but allows for free-flowing conversation.
  • The primary data is the group discussion itself—the interactions, agreements, and disagreements between participants.

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths: Captures group dynamics, generates rich data quickly, flexible, and can be empowering for participants.

Weaknesses: A dominant personality can sway the group, the moderator can unintentionally bias the results, and the data is not easily generalizable.

Ethical Dilemmas in the Field

Field research presents unique and complex ethical challenges because the researcher is so deeply immersed in people's lives.

Deception

Is it ever acceptable to conduct covert research without telling people they are being studied? This is a major ethical debate with strong arguments on both sides.

Confidentiality

Protecting the identities of people and the specific location of the field site is crucial, especially when studying sensitive or illegal activities.

Involvement in Illegal Behavior

What should a researcher do if they witness illegal acts? This creates a conflict between being a neutral observer and a responsible citizen.

Power and Relationships

The researcher is in a position of power. They must be careful not to exploit the trust and relationships they build in the field for personal gain.
